The verse highlights how the believers, initially reluctant to fight, were made to face their enemies in battle. Initially, they had disagreed over the division of war spoils, which Allah had taken away from them, only for it to be fairly distributed by His Messenger. They also disliked the prospect of meeting the armed Quraysh, who had come to protect their caravan, but Allah decreed that they would meet the enemy in battle instead, bringing them victory and guidance. Despite their hesitation, this battle was a means for Islam to prevail and for Allah’s will to manifest, showing that what is disliked may often hold greater benefits, while what is liked may lead to harm. The Muslims, led by the Prophet Muhammad, were encouraged by the words of their companions, including Al-Miqdad and Sa'd bin Mu'adh, who pledged to fight alongside him. The Prophet, inspired by their support, assured them of victory and proceeded with the march, knowing that Allah had promised either the spoils of the caravan or victory over the Quraysh army.
