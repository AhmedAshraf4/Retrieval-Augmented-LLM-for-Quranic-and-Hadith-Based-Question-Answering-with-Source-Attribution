Allah commanded the believers to fast, abstaining from food, drink, and sexual activity, with the intent of purifying the soul and cleansing it from harmful behaviors. Fasting was made obligatory for Muslims, just as it was for previous nations, to encourage obedience. Initially, those who found fasting difficult, such as the elderly, had the option to feed a poor person for each day they did not fast. However, this was later abrogated, and fasting became compulsory for all those who could do so. The exception was made for the elderly, pregnant, or breastfeeding women who feared harm to themselves or their children; they could feed the poor instead of fasting without needing to make up the missed days. Fasting serves as a shield and brings greater benefit than feeding the poor, though feeding an additional person as charity is considered virtuous. The specific days of fasting were fixed, and the obligation was further clarified in later verses.
