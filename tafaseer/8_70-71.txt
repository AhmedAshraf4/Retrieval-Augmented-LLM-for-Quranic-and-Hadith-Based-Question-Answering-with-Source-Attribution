Before the Battle of Badr, the Prophet Muhammad learned that some people from Bani Hashim, including his uncle Al-Abbas, had been forced to join the pagan army, unwilling to fight. He instructed his followers not to kill them if encountered. Despite this, Abu Hudhayfah expressed a desire to kill his own family members if they were on the opposing side, which led to a confrontation with Umar. The Prophet later heard Al-Abbas in pain due to his shackles and ordered his release. When the pagans sought to ransom their prisoners, Al-Abbas asked to be ransomed with his wealth buried earlier, but the Prophet rejected the offer, explaining that the spoils from him belonged to the Muslims. Al-Abbas later ransomed himself, his nephews, and an ally, fulfilling his obligations. In response, Allah revealed a verse promising that if the captives had goodness in their hearts, they would receive better than what was taken from them, and Allah would forgive them. Al-Abbas, after becoming a Muslim, was blessed with more than he had lost. The verse also addressed the possibility of betrayal, stating that if the captives intended to betray the Prophet, they had already betrayed Allah before, and Allah, in His wisdom, allowed the Muslims to capture them at Badr.
