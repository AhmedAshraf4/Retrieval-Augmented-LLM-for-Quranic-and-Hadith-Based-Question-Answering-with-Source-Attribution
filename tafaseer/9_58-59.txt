Some hypocrites accused the Prophet of unfairness in distributing alms, questioning his integrity despite their own lack of fairness. They sought more for themselves rather than defending the religion. When they were given alms, they were pleased, but when they were not, they became angry. A Bedouin who had recently embraced Islam criticized the Prophet’s fairness in distributing gold and silver, leading the Prophet to warn that similar individuals would emerge in his Ummah, people who recite the Qur'an but whose faith does not go beyond their throats, and who should be killed if they rise against the Muslims. The Prophet also reminded that he was only a keeper of alms, not the one who gives or withholds them. The hypocrites' behavior was contrasted with the ideal conduct of being content with what Allah and His Messenger give, trusting in Allah’s bounty, and hoping in Him alone for enrichment. This conduct was emphasized as the path of obedience to the Messenger, following his commands, avoiding his prohibitions, and adhering to his guidance.
